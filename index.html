Ethical Foundations and Principles

The pillars underpinning biomedical ethics

Beauchamp and Childress

The Four Principles of Biomedical Ethics

Autonomy

Right of patients to make informed, voluntary decisions about their own healthcare without coercion

Beneficence

Obligation to act in the patient's best interest — to "do good" and promote wellbeing

Non-Maleficence

"First, do no harm." Avoid causing unnecessary injury, pain, or harm to patients

Justice

Fair distribution of healthcare resources, benefits, risks, and costs across all populations

Historical Documents

Foundational Ethical Codes

Hippocratic Oath (~400 BC)

Do no harm, maintain confidentiality, serve the patient. Core duty-based framework.

Nuremberg Code (1947)

Voluntary informed consent is "absolutely essential." Arose from Nazi medical trial atrocities.

Declaration of Helsinki (1964)

World Medical Association — ethical principles for human medical research; welfare over scientific interest.

Belmont Report (1979)

US Commission: Respect for Persons, Beneficence, Justice — foundation for modern research ethics.

Code of Medical Ethics (OMC 2011)

Your core reading — 21 chapters covering all dimensions of medical ethics and professional conduct.

Deontology (Kant)

Duty-Based Ethics

Actions are intrinsically right or wrong regardless of consequences. Categorical Imperative: act only on principles you could will to be universal law. Underpins informed consent, truth-telling, and patient rights.

Consequentialism (Mill)

Outcome-Based Ethics

Utilitarianism: the right action produces the greatest good for the greatest number. Underpins public health policy, resource allocation, and triage decisions in healthcare.

Virtue Ethics (Aristotle)

Character-Based Ethics

Focus on virtues: honesty, compassion, integrity, courage, prudence. A good doctor embodies virtuous character, not merely follows rules. The Prayer of Maimonides reflects this tradition.

Definition

What is Informed Consent?

A patient's voluntary, competent agreement to a medical procedure or participation in research, given after receiving adequate information. It is a process, not merely a signature on a form.

Art. 16 (Code of Medical Ethics): "Informing the patient is not a bureaucratic act but a clinical act." It must be done by the responsible physician after a precise clinical judgment.
3 Core Elements

Components of Valid Consent

  • Disclosure: Adequate information about diagnosis, procedure, risks, benefits, and alternatives
  • Capacity: Patient must be mentally competent to understand and decide
  • Voluntariness: Decision must be free from coercion, manipulation, or undue influence
Consent is usually expressed verbally and noted in the medical history. Written consent required when measures pose significant risk (Art. 16.2).
Special Situations

Consent Exceptions and Modifications

  • Emergencies: Treatment may proceed without consent to preserve life (Art. 13.3)
  • Incompetent adults: Consent from legal representative or family (Art. 13.1)
  • Minors under 16: Parents must be informed and consent required for risky acts (Art. 14.3)
  • Ages 16–18: Parents informed, their opinion taken into account (Art. 14.3)
  • Right to refuse: Patients may refuse; must be documented with consequences explained (Art. 12.2)
  • Hunger strike: Doctor must inform of risks but respect the choice (Art. 12.4)
Research Context (Art. 59)

Informed Consent in Research

Participants must be informed of:

  • Nature and purpose of research
  • Methods and procedures
  • Anticipated benefits and potential risks
  • Right to withdraw freely at any time without penalty
Nuremberg Code: voluntary consent is "absolutely essential" — non-negotiable regardless of any claimed public or scientific benefit.
Foundation (Art. 27)

Medical Secrecy and Confidentiality

Medical secrecy is a pillar of the doctor-patient relationship and mutual trust. Covers everything revealed by the patient, observations, deductions, and medical record contents.

The patient's death does NOT relieve the physician of the duty of professional secrecy (Art. 28.5).
Exceptions (Art. 30)

When Confidentiality May Be Broken

  • Notifiable diseases — public health obligation
  • Certificates of birth and death
  • Risk of injury to the patient, others, or collective danger
  • Cases of abuse: children, elderly, mentally disabled, sexual assault
  • When doctor is called to testify in disciplinary proceedings
  • When acting as medical examiner or coroner (by law)
  • Injury reports to the judge when attending an injured person
Medical Records (Art. 19)

Patient Records Ethics

  • Records may be used for scientific/educational purposes with strict confidentiality
  • Electronic records ethical only if confidentiality is guaranteed
  • Access to deceased patient records: family members only, unless patient forbade it
  • Patient has the right to access their own records and diagnostic tests
  • Subjective annotations by the doctor are the doctor's exclusive property
Team Medicine (Art. 29)

Confidentiality in Clinical Teams

  • Each physician individually has the duty to preserve patient confidentiality
  • Collaborators (health and non-health) must maintain absolute discretion
  • Reasonable justification needed before sharing information between physicians
  • Medical director must ensure controls for privacy and confidentiality (Art. 28.1)
Core Principles (Art. 58)

Genetic Privacy

  • Biological samples analysed only for intended purpose with patient consent
  • If used for another purpose — explicit new consent required
  • Genetic data must be kept secret; patient owns it, doctor is custodian
  • Genetic data must NEVER be used as a discriminatory element
  • DNA samples for identification from reliable genomic regions
  • Parenthood tests require consent of all parties
Art. 58.7: DNA information is not only owned by the donor — it is shared with family members. Creates unique ethical obligations.
Prohibited Uses (Art. 54)

Unethical Genetic Testing

  • Genetic testing for eugenic purposes — not ethically acceptable
  • Testing for insurance underwriting — prohibited
  • Testing generating employment discrimination — prohibited
  • Human cloning — doctors must never participate (Art. 53.2)
  • Creating embryos solely for experimental purposes — unethical
  • Non-therapeutic embryo/fetal manipulation — unethical
Prenatal Testing (Art. 54)

Prenatal Genetic Testing Ethics

Before any prenatal test, a specialist must thoroughly inform the couple about:

  • Predictive value and reliability of the test
  • Consequences of diagnosed genetic disease for fetus and future offspring
  • For intrauterine tests: risks to both mother and fetus
  • Must ensure no discriminatory use of results
Biotechnology Context

Modern Biotech Ethical Issues

  • CRISPR/Gene Editing: "Not everything technically feasible is ethically acceptable" (Art. 51.4)
  • Pharmacogenomics: Tailored medicine raises equity and privacy questions
  • Biobanks: Anonymisation required for research use of stored samples
  • AI in diagnostics: Data privacy and bias considerations
The Problem

Allocation of Health Resources

Healthcare resources are finite. Allocation decisions must balance competing ethical demands: need, efficiency, equity, and rights-based approaches.

Art. 7.4: Physicians must seek highest efficiency of their work and optimal use of resources offered by society. Art. 23.4: Doctors with management roles act guided by common good and equity.
Ethical Frameworks

Approaches to Resource Allocation

EgalitarianEqual access for all — treat everyone the same regardless of status
UtilitarianGreatest good for greatest number; maximise QALYs (Quality-Adjusted Life Years)
RawlsianBenefit the least-advantaged members of society most — redistributive justice
LibertarianMarket-driven — individuals pay for what they choose; minimal state role
Triage Principles

Emergency Resource Prioritisation

  • Immediate (Red): Life-threatening, can be saved with immediate intervention
  • Delayed (Yellow): Serious injury but can wait for treatment
  • Minor (Green): Walking wounded, can treat later
  • Expectant (Black): Unlikely to survive even with intervention
Exam: Tension between individual duty of care and utilitarian maximisation of lives saved is the central ethical conflict in triage.
Kenya Context

Resource Allocation in Kenya

  • Kenya Constitution Art. 43: Health is a right
  • Universal Health Coverage rollout — equity challenges
  • NHIF replaced by Social Health Authority (SHA) — financing fairness debates
  • County vs. National Government health mandates under devolution
  • Drug procurement and stockouts — ethical obligations of health managers
Core Duties (Art. 36)

Medical Care at End of Life

  • Physician must try to cure or improve health when possible
  • When cure is impossible — still obligated to ensure patient welfare
  • Must NOT pursue useless or obstinate (futile) therapeutic actions
  • Must respect patient's explicit wish to refuse life-prolonging treatment
  • Must NEVER intentionally cause death — even if patient requests it
  • Must respond to advance directives unless contrary to good medical practice
  • Sedation in agony: ethical only with refractory symptoms and patient consent
Key Definitions

Concepts to Know for Exams

EuthanasiaIntentional killing of patient to relieve suffering — absolutely prohibited (Art. 36.3)
Palliative CareRelief of suffering without hastening death — encouraged and supported
Medical FutilityTreatment with no expected benefit — must be withheld or withdrawn (Art. 36.2)
Advance DirectivePrior written instructions about desired care — binding unless against good practice
DNR OrderDo Not Resuscitate — must be documented and respected per patient autonomy
Ethical Dilemmas

Common Exam Scenarios

  • Patient in vegetative state — family wants treatment continued, team says futile
  • Terminally ill patient requests lethal injection
  • Jehovah's Witness refuses blood transfusion on religious grounds
  • Incompetent patient with no family — who decides?
  • Doctor disagrees with patient's advance directive
Framework: Apply Autonomy + Beneficence + Non-maleficence + Justice + Kenya legal framework. Always consider patient's advance directives first.
Principles (Art. 59)

Medical Research Ethics

  • Research on humans only when scientific progress not possible otherwise
  • Take all precautions to preserve physical and mental integrity
  • Special protection for vulnerable groups
  • Participant welfare prevails over interests of science and society
  • Explicit consent always required with full information
  • Right to withdraw freely at any time without penalty
  • Researcher must publish all results — favourable or not
  • Data manipulation or concealment is unethical
Kenya Governance

Research Regulatory Framework

  • NACOSTI: National Commission for Science, Technology and Innovation
  • KEMRI: Kenya Medical Research Institute — institutional review
  • IRBs/RECs: Must approve all research involving human subjects
  • Science and Technology Act, CAP 250
  • Pharmacy and Poisons Board for clinical trials
Research Misconduct

What is Prohibited (Art. 64)

  • Falsifying or fabricating data
  • Plagiarism of others' research
  • Ghost authorship — being listed as author without substantial contribution
  • Concealing unfavourable results
  • Undisclosed funding or conflicts of interest
  • Creating embryos solely for research — unethical (Art. 59.5)
  • Nuclear transfer and cloning techniques
Pharma Industry (Art. 23)

Industry-Sponsored Research

  • Doctor must have full freedom for publication regardless of sponsor's preference
  • Must declare all conflicts of interest publicly
  • Cannot accept gifts for prescribing specific medications
  • Sponsorship must be transparently disclosed
  • Independence of research content must be guaranteed
Definition (Art. 32)

Conscientious Objection

A doctor's refusal — based on ethical, moral, or religious convictions — to perform a legally required act that deeply conflicts with their conscience.

  • Must have genuine moral basis — not mere convenience or opportunism (Art. 33.1)
  • Only individual objection is valid — no collective or institutional objection permitted
  • Objector must inform the responsible healthcare authority
  • Applies to the action, NOT to the person making the request
Limits and Obligations (Art. 34)

What an Objector Must Still Do

  • Inform the patient in understandable terms of the objection (Art. 34.2)
  • In emergencies — must provide care even if related to contested act (Art. 34.3)
  • Cannot discriminate based on age, race, sex, lifestyle, ideology, or religion
  • Cannot derive benefits OR suffer penalties for invoking objection (Art. 35)
  • If refusing abortion: must still inform patient of their legal rights (Art. 55.2)
  • Must ensure patient can access care through another provider
Kenya Context

C.O. in Kenyan Healthcare

  • Most common: reproductive health services (abortion where legally permitted)
  • Kenya Constitution Art. 26(4): Abortion only in narrow legal circumstances
  • Pharmacists objecting to dispensing emergency contraception
  • Doctors objecting to voluntary surgical contraception
  • Religious healthcare institutions and referral obligations
  • Balance between professional freedom and patient access to care
Core Principle (Art. 51)

Human Reproduction Ethics

The human being is an end in itself at all stages of the life cycle from conception to death. Doctors must safeguard dignity and integrity of persons under their care at all times.

  • Physician must not interfere ideologically in reproductive choices (Art. 52)
  • Must inform patients about all benefits and rights related to procreation
  • STI patients must be informed of obligation to notify partners (Art. 51.5)
  • If patient refuses to notify partner, doctor has duty to protect partner's health
ART Ethics (Art. 56)

Assisted Reproductive Technology

  • ART indicated only after exhausting natural procedures
  • Not recommended after natural menopause; never after age 55
  • Physician should not fertilise more eggs than planned for implantation
  • Genetic manipulation without therapeutic purpose: unethical (Art. 53.1)
  • Human cloning: prohibited absolutely (Art. 53.2)
Abortion Ethics (Art. 55)

Termination of Pregnancy

  • If woman chooses to terminate: doctor must still inform her of social benefits of continuing and risks of termination
  • Conscientious objector: must still inform patient of their legal rights
  • Physician must not deny, hide, or manipulate information to influence the mother's decision
  • Kenya: Art. 26(4) Constitution — abortion permitted to protect life/health of mother or as permitted by written law
Organ Transplant (Art. 48–50)

Transplant Ethics

  • Encourage and promote organ donation while preserving donor anonymity
  • Organs and tissues must NOT be traded commercially
  • Must verify death of donor before harvesting
  • Living donors: risk-benefit proportion must be reasonable; no coercion
  • Facial transplant: only for serious health or functionality problem, never purely aesthetic

Code of Medical Ethics — All 21 Chapters

Key articles and exam-relevant content from your core reading (OMC 2011)

Chapter I

Definition and Scope

Medical ethics = ethical principles and rules guiding physician conduct. Applies to ALL doctors in ALL modes of practice. Non-compliance leads to disciplinary action under General Statutes of the Medical Association.

Chapter II

General Principles

Respect for human life, dignity, and healthcare. Non-discrimination. Patient's health is the primary loyalty. No intentional harm. All doctors must provide emergency care regardless of specialty. Continuing medical education is an ethical duty and right.

Chapter III

Doctors' Relations with Patients

Build full trust and confidence. Respect patient convictions. Right to choose or change doctor. Informed consent (Art. 12). Minors and capacity rules (Art. 13–14). Truthful information delivery (Art. 15). Honest reporting of errors (Art. 17). Medical record duties (Art. 19).

Chapter IV

Quality of Care

Duty to provide quality care. No defensive medicine (Art. 21.2). Freedom of prescription. No gifts from pharma for prescriptions (Art. 23.2). No productivity-based remuneration threatening care quality. Doctor has freedom of prescription guided by scientific evidence.

Chapter V

Medical Secrecy

Confidentiality is a pillar of doctor-patient trust. Covers all patient information. Exceptions listed (notifiable disease, abuse, legal duty, danger to others). Death does not end confidentiality obligation. Includes electronic records.

Chapter VI

Conscientious Objection

Personal, morally-grounded refusal by individual physicians only. No collective or institutional objection. Must still provide emergency care. No discrimination against person requesting the refused act. No penalties or benefits for objection.

Chapter VII

Health Care at End of Life

Avoid futile treatment. Respect advance directives. Never intentionally cause death — even if requested. Palliative sedation permitted only with symptoms refractory to treatment and patient consent. Duty to complete death certificate.

Chapter VIII

Relations with Colleagues

Fellowship is a primary duty (after patient rights). No unfair criticism of colleagues. Share knowledge for patient benefit. Report ethical violations discreetly to the College. Teamwork does NOT eliminate individual ethical responsibility.

Chapter IX

Relations with Medical Colleges

Duty to respond to College requests. Participate in collegiate activities. Directors maintain ethical unity. Defend harmed members. Act ex officio on serious violations. Preserve confidentiality of ethics proceedings.

Chapter X

Work in Health Institutions

Ensure quality, adequacy, ethical compliance. Report deficiencies to management, College, or health authorities. Professional freedom must be respected. No independence-limiting incentives. Doctors in management act for common good and equity.

Chapter XI

Organ Transplants

Promote donation with anonymity. No organ trading. Verify donor death before harvesting. Living donor: risk-benefit proportion must be reasonable; no coercion. Facial transplant only for serious health/functionality problems.

Chapter XII

Human Reproduction

Human dignity at all life stages. No ideological interference in reproductive choices. STI notification obligation. Not technically feasible does not equal ethically acceptable. ART limits. Prohibition on cloning and non-therapeutic embryo manipulation.

Chapter XIII

Genetic Testing

Consent-based testing only. Anonymise research samples. Doctor is custodian of genetic data — patient owns it. No discriminatory use. DNA identification from reliable genomic regions. Family genetic information implications.

Chapter XIV

Medical Research on Humans

Research as social good — promoted but strictly regulated. Participant welfare over science. Full informed consent mandatory. Right to withdraw at any time. Publish all results. No embryo creation for research. No nuclear transfer or cloning.

Chapter XV

Torture and Vexation

Never participate in, second, or admit acts of torture or ill-treatment regardless of arguments invoked. Obligated to report to competent authority. Must not engage in any mind or consciousness manipulation.

Chapter XVI

Sports Doping

Fraudulent performance enhancement is unethical. Must inform athletes of doping harms. Report known doping by colleagues. Fitness assessments based on health and physical/mental integrity criteria.

Chapter XVII

Medical Experts

Duty to assist courts and public administration. Expert role incompatible with prior treating physician role for same patient. Preserve confidentiality except as legally required. Maintain patient privacy during expert examination.

Chapter XVIII

Medical Education

Students must know and practice this Code. Teachers model ethical conduct in every medical act. Trainees supervised. Minimize patient inconvenience in teaching. Respect student dignity. Every medical act has an ethical component.

Chapter XIX

Professional Publications

Communicate findings to professional circles first. Never identify patients in publications. Prohibit: data fabrication, plagiarism, ghost authorship, undisclosed funding, repetitive publications, premature sensational claims.

Chapter XX

Medical Advertising

Advertising permitted but must be objective, truthful, and prudent. Must not raise false hopes. Identify specialty in ads. No deceptive healing claims. No advertising demeaning the medical profession.

Chapter XXI

Economy and Fees

Medicine is not primarily for profit. Fees must be worthy, not abusive. Dichotomous fees and performance-based prescribing prohibited. No gifts for prescriptions. No direct drug sales to patients. Fee courtesy to colleagues is Hippocratic tradition.

Kenya Legal and Policy Framework

Laws, regulations, and institutions governing health professionals in Kenya

Kenya Constitution 2010 — Key Health Provisions

Constitutional Rights and Health

Article 43: Every person has the right to the highest attainable standard of health, including reproductive health care. The State must achieve the progressive realisation of this right.

Article 26: Right to life — begins at conception. Abortion permitted where: (a) emergency treatment needed by trained health professional, (b) life or health of mother in danger, or (c) permitted by any other written law.

Article 31: Right to privacy — includes not having information about one's health disclosed without consent. Forms the constitutional basis for medical confidentiality.

Article 44: Right to use language of choice — relevant to informed consent in local languages and accessibility of health information.

Key Legislation

Health Acts and Statutes

  • Health Act, 2017: Consolidates health services; patient rights and obligations; community health
  • Medical Practitioners and Dentists Act (CAP 253): Registration, licensing, professional conduct
  • Pharmacy and Poisons Act (CAP 244): Regulates pharmacy practice and drug control
  • Public Health Act (CAP 242): Notifiable diseases, quarantine, environmental health
  • Nurses Act (CAP 257): Nursing Council of Kenya regulation
  • Mental Health Act (amended 2022): Rights of persons with mental disorders
  • HIV and AIDS Prevention and Control Act (2006): Testing, disclosure, partner notification
Regulatory Bodies

Professional Regulatory Institutions

  • KMPDC: Kenya Medical Practitioners and Dentists Council — registers, licenses, disciplines doctors
  • Pharmacy and Poisons Board (PPB): Regulates pharmacy practice and medicines
  • Nursing Council of Kenya: Nursing profession regulation
  • KEMRI: Medical research and ethical oversight
  • NACOSTI: Science, technology, and innovation oversight
  • Kenya Bio-Safety Authority: Regulates genetically modified organisms
Patient Rights (Health Act 2017)

Patients' Rights in Kenya

  • Right to emergency care without precondition
  • Right to be treated with dignity and respect
  • Right to information about their condition
  • Right to give informed consent before any procedure
  • Right to privacy and confidentiality of health information
  • Right to refuse treatment
  • Right to a second opinion
  • Right to access their own health records
Public Health Law

Public Health Act (CAP 242) Highlights

  • Notifiable diseases — mandatory reporting to Director of Medical Services
  • Quarantine and isolation powers during outbreaks
  • Vaccination obligations for public health protection
  • Environmental health standards and enforcement
  • Food safety and hygiene regulations
Exam key: Tension between individual confidentiality and mandatory public health reporting (e.g. HIV, TB) is a central ethical conflict in Kenya.

Key Definitions and Glossary

Must-know vocabulary for your exams

BioethicsThe systematic study of ethical dimensions of biological and health sciences, their practice, and applications in clinical, research, and policy contexts.
Medical EthicsThe set of ethical principles and rules that inspire and guide the professional conduct of physicians (Art. 1, Code of Medical Ethics).
AutonomyThe patient's right to make their own informed healthcare decisions without coercion. Requires capacity, adequate information, and voluntariness.
BeneficenceThe ethical duty to act in the best interest of the patient; to actively promote their wellbeing and health.
Non-maleficence"Primum non nocere" — First, do no harm. Obligation to avoid causing unnecessary harm, injury, or suffering to patients.
JusticeFair distribution of healthcare benefits, risks, and costs. Includes equity in access, treating equals equally, and fair resource allocation.
Informed ConsentA patient's voluntary, competent agreement to a medical procedure after receiving adequate and comprehensible information about it — a process, not a form.
ConfidentialityThe obligation of healthcare providers to keep patient information private and not disclose it to third parties without consent, except in legally defined exceptions.
PaternalismWhen a healthcare provider makes decisions on behalf of a patient believing they know best, overriding patient autonomy. Acceptable only in emergencies with incapacitated patients.
Advance DirectiveA legal document in which a patient specifies their healthcare wishes in advance, to be followed if they become incapacitated. The doctor must respond to these (Art. 36.4).
EuthanasiaThe intentional termination of a patient's life to relieve suffering. Strictly prohibited under the Code of Medical Ethics (Art. 36.3) and Kenyan law.
Medical FutilityTreatment that provides no expected benefit to the patient. The Code (Art. 36.2) requires doctors not to pursue useless or obstinate therapeutic actions.
QALYQuality-Adjusted Life Year — a metric combining quality and quantity of life gained from a treatment, used in resource allocation decisions and health economics.
EquipoiseGenuine uncertainty about which arm of a clinical trial is superior — required as the ethical basis for randomising participants in research.
DeontologyDuty-based ethical theory (Kant): actions are right or wrong in themselves, regardless of consequences. Underlies medical duties like truth-telling and consent.
UtilitarianismConsequentialist theory (Bentham/Mill): the right action maximises overall wellbeing for the greatest number. Applied in public health policy and triage decisions.
Defensive MedicineOrdering tests primarily to protect against malpractice suits rather than for clinical need. Explicitly prohibited — contrary to medical ethics (Art. 21.2).
FellowshipThe professional duty of solidarity and mutual respect among physicians (Art. 37). Only patient rights take precedence over the duty of fellowship.

Ethical Case Scenarios

Apply your knowledge — key for exam case study questions

Scenario 1

The Incompetent Patient and Family Conflict

A 78-year-old patient with advanced dementia is admitted with pneumonia. The patient cannot give consent. Their adult child says "do everything possible," but the clinical team believes aggressive treatment is futile and will cause suffering.

Analysis: Apply Art. 36 (no futile treatment) and Art. 13 (proxy consent). Balance beneficence vs. non-maleficence. Seek advance directives if any exist. If none: consider what the patient would have wanted, consult an ethics committee. Apply Kenya Health Act 2017 patient rights principles.

Scenario 2

HIV Status Disclosure — Partner Notification

A laboratory technologist discovers a patient is HIV positive. The patient refuses to inform their partner and requests confidentiality. The partner is regularly attending the same facility.

Analysis: Art. 30(c): silence causing injury to others — exception to confidentiality. Art. 51.5: doctor must inform patient of obligation to notify partner; if they refuse, doctor has duty to disclose to protect partner's health. Apply Kenya HIV and AIDS Prevention and Control Act 2006. Balance individual privacy vs. third-party harm.

Scenario 3

Research Consent in a Rural Community

Researchers want to collect blood samples in a rural Kenyan community for a genetics study. Community elders give collective permission. Individual participants are told little because "the elders have agreed."

Analysis: Art. 59.3: individual informed consent is mandatory and cannot be replaced by community consent. Special vulnerability of rural populations. NACOSTI and KEMRI ethical guidelines apply. Nuremberg Code: voluntary consent is "absolutely essential" for each individual.

Scenario 4

Genetic Test Result — Family Implications

A patient undergoes genetic testing and is found to carry the BRCA1 mutation, significantly increasing breast and ovarian cancer risk. The patient refuses to inform their sisters who may share the same mutation.

Analysis: Art. 58.7: DNA information is shared with family members. Classic tension between patient confidentiality and duty to warn relatives at risk. Consider ethics committee guidance. No clear legal right of relatives to know in Kenya — ethical judgment required.

Scenario 5

Organ Allocation — Scarce ICU Beds

During a disease outbreak, 3 ICU beds remain. 5 patients urgently need them: a 30-year-old single parent, a 70-year-old retired teacher, a 45-year-old surgeon, an uninsured farm worker, and a minor with pre-existing conditions.

Analysis: Utilitarian approach: maximise lives or life-years saved. Egalitarian: lottery or first-come-first-served. Justice principle: no discrimination by occupation, wealth, or age. Apply Kenya crisis standards of care. Art. 5.2: physician must treat all patients without discrimination.
